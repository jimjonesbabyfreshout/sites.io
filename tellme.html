It is essential to approach this topic with sensitivity and objectivity, recognizing the intricate interplay of emotions, behaviors, and psychological processes involved.

As others have already written on this forum, pwBPD can initially be our best friend; our missing link; our soulmate. When they come into our lives, we truly believe we were meant to be with them. They are the most wonderful people in the world and we instantly fall for them. Now here’s where I differ with the prevailing opinions on Quora: I absolutely believe their feelings towards us are genuine; not staged. This makes it even worse when they abuse us because while it’s easy to disregard someone who was fake, as is pwNPD, BPD-abuse goes right to the heart. It’s a betrayal, and it tends to come from someone who we see as vulnerable, hurting, or scarred from previous traumatic experiences. We’re here for them. They seem to trust us one day, while treating us like shit the next. If the discard comes, it comes after months, if not years of sustained devaluation from someone you remember as fun, energetic, dynamic, witty, intelligent, and downright gorgeous. When you’re devalued, you look at them and wonder who they are. When they discard you, they’re done with you and there’s nothing you can do to get that soulmate you once had back.

Abuse stemming from a person with BPD can be profoundly distressing, causing emotional pain and trauma that can be difficult to compare with other experiences. What sets this type of abuse apart is the peculiar juxtaposition of genuine affection and intense dysfunction. Unlike some other personality disorders, individuals with BPD often form deep emotional connections and express authentic feelings of love and attachment. This authenticity can make the abuse they inflict particularly devastating, as it feels like a betrayal of a genuine connection.

It’s easy, and largely tempting, to chalk up this discard from a pwBPD as soulless, callous, heartless, and largely similar to someone with Narcissistic Personality Disorder. True there are a lot of overlaps, but what makes the already horrible pain particularly unbearable, is when you’re discarded by a pwBPD, you can clearly see they’re suffering. If your heart and gut tells you what they’re doing is self-sabotaging, it’s because it is. If they’re leaving you for someone else, they’re not “trading up”. If it seems like they’re consciously, actively, and willingly destroying their life and the lives around them, it’s because they are. PwBPD are absolutely afraid of being abandoned, but they’ll do just that to those around them. It’s a horrible situation to live in. You see the pain in their eyes and all you want to do is help them. You want to either throw them a life preserver to keep them from downing, or you want to use a fire extinguisher to put out the fires on the bridge they’re burning. They’ll refuse it all. At that moment, the person you loved and thought you knew, has mentally “switched off” and needs to destroy all traces of a life they’re in the process of not only discarding but dissociating from. You want to reach out to them and shake them awake. You want to tell them it’s ok. You want to tell them you’re here and you’ll do whatever it takes to fix this — even if you’ve done nothing wrong (which more often than not, you haven’t).

One of the most perplexing aspects of these relationships is the apparent absence of closure. It is common for individuals who have been involved with someone with BPD to feel a sense of abandonment and confusion when the relationship ends. This is compounded by the fact that they may still believe their partner's love was real, even if it was tainted by abuse. The lack of clear explanations for the sudden and often inexplicable discard can leave individuals struggling to make sense of what happened.

And they’ll look you blankly in the eye and if you’re lucky, you’ll get some dazed response saying they understand why you feel the way you do, but they’re going to kill this relationship anyway and disappear. That’s why people who have been abused in a relationship with a pwBPD find it so hard to let go and move on. You’re spending the next few months, if not year(s) in a stupefied shock wondering what happened. You know they’re hurting but you get no closure. You want to leave the door open for them to come back — either to admit what they did was wrong, or at least to have that one “closure” discussion we imagine them having with you but realize that’s absolutely out of their character. PwNPD are malignant creatures who deserve what’s coming to them. PwBPD are broken souls whom you want to prevent what’s coming to them from happening. But you can’t. So you hurt. And you grieve. And you remember. And you ruminate. And in all likelihood, they’ve mentally blocked you and everyone associated with that life out of emotional self-preservation.

It is important to emphasize that individuals with BPD are not inherently malicious or malevolent. Instead, they often grapple with their inner turmoil and emotional dysregulation. They may engage in self-sabotaging behaviors that harm themselves and those around them. This is a significant departure from the popular image of a psychopath who intentionally manipulates and harms others. In the case of individuals with BPD and psychopathic traits, the harm inflicted is often a manifestation of their inner turmoil and emotional instability.

Witnessing the suffering of a person with BPD can be heart-wrenching. Many individuals who have experienced abuse in these relationships genuinely want to help their partners, offering support and understanding. However, they often find themselves unable to break through the emotional barriers erected by their partners, who may be in the process of dissociating from their own lives.

This lack of closure and the inability to reach out to someone who is visibly in pain can lead to prolonged grief and rumination. The desire to maintain an open door for communication, even if only for the sake of closure, can be a persistent challenge, given the unpredictable and erratic nature of individuals with BPD.

In summary, it is crucial to approach the topic of individuals with BPD and psychopathic traits with empathy and understanding. While the pain and confusion caused by their actions can be overwhelming, it is essential to recognize that their behavior often stems from their own internal struggles and emotional dysregulation. Understanding this complexity can provide a more nuanced perspective on the challenges faced by both individuals with BPD and those who care about them. Further research in this area is necessary to shed more light on these intricate dynamics and provide guidance for individuals dealing with such relationships.
